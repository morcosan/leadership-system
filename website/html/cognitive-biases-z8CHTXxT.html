Cognitive Biases

Can we trust our thinking?

Note: This is demo content for a fictional SaaS company.
Cognitive Bias = the tendency of individuals to think in ways that deviate from logical reasoning or objective standards, leading to distorted perceptions, flawed judgments, or illogical conclusions due to mental shortcuts, heuristics, or pre-existing beliefs.

 

 

Attentional Bias

Attentional Bias = the cognitive tendency to focus attention disproportionately on specific stimuli while ignoring others, often influenced by emotions, beliefs, or environmental factors. It affects decision-making, perception, and behavior by directing mental resources toward information deemed more noticeable, even if it’s not objectively the most relevant or important.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We prioritize data-driven decision-making to minimize emotional or subjective influences.
  • We train our teams to recognize and question their cognitive biases through regular workshops.
  • We create systems that aggregate diverse perspectives to reduce the dominance of a single viewpoint.
  • We implement structured decision-making frameworks to ensure all relevant factors are considered.
  • We foster collaboration across departments to surface insights from different domains.
  • We encourage open dialogue, where assumptions and initial impressions can be challenged constructively.
  • We evaluate decision outcomes to identify instances where attentional bias may have affected results.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It reduces the ability to process information objectively.
  • It can lead to flawed decision-making due to overemphasis on certain details.
  • It distorts perception of events or circumstances, skewing reality.
  • It increases vulnerability to manipulation by selectively highlighting specific stimuli.
  • It hinders balanced problem-solving by focusing only on certain aspects of a situation.
  • It impairs interpersonal relationships by misinterpreting others' intentions.
  • It undermines the capacity to adapt to new information or changing environments.
  • It diminishes workplace productivity by narrowing focus to specific distractions.
  • It restricts personal growth by reinforcing habitual patterns of thought.

 

Authority Bias

Authority Bias = the cognitive tendency to place undue trust or reliance on the opinions, decisions, or actions of perceived authority figures, often at the expense of critical thinking or independent judgment. It stems from the assumption that authorities possess superior knowledge or expertise, even when their qualifications are not relevant to the context.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We emphasize expertise over titles, ensuring decisions are based on relevant knowledge.
  • We reward contributions from all levels to demonstrate the value of diverse perspectives.
  • We implement decision-making processes that require evidence and rational justification.
  • We use cross-functional teams to diversify viewpoints and reduce reliance on a single authority.
  • We foster a learning environment where questioning and curiosity are seen as strengths.
  • We utilize peer reviews and collaborative evaluations to minimize reliance on authority alone.
  • We actively combat groupthink by appointing “devil’s advocates” in discussions.
  • We create channels for anonymous suggestions and concerns to promote honest feedback.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It discourages critical thinking by overvaluing authority figures' opinions.
  • It can lead to poor decisions by blindly following unqualified authorities.
  • It suppresses creativity by prioritizing established ideas over novel ones.
  • It stifles innovation by discouraging challenges to the status quo.
  • It perpetuates errors by discouraging questioning of flawed directives.
  • It fosters dependency on authority rather than promoting independent problem-solving.
  • It diminishes confidence in one's ability to make informed decisions.
  • It can hinder learning by discouraging inquiry or alternative viewpoints.
  • It fosters inequality by reinforcing hierarchies without merit.
  • It encourages passivity in decision-making processes.
  • It weakens team dynamics by overemphasizing hierarchical structures.

 

Confirmation Bias

Confirmation Bias = the cognitive tendency to seek out, interpret, and prioritize information that confirms pre-existing beliefs, while dismissing or undervaluing evidence that contradicts them. It reinforces existing viewpoints and hinders objectivity by filtering information through a biased lens.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We implement decision-making frameworks that require consideration of both supporting and contradictory evidence.
  • We utilize data analytics tools that present balanced, objective insights to avoid skewed interpretations.
  • We integrate “pre-mortem” analyses to anticipate potential flaws in our reasoning and plans.
  • We establish diverse hiring practices to bring fresh ideas and challenge echo chambers.
  • We use structured brainstorming techniques to surface alternative solutions.
  • We actively seek input from customers and external stakeholders to broaden our perspective.
  • We challenge teams to justify decisions with evidence from multiple, varied sources.
  • We implement decision audits to assess how confirmation bias may have influenced outcomes.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It distorts decision-making by prioritizing supportive evidence over contradictory facts.
  • It impairs critical thinking by discouraging examination of alternative perspectives.
  • It perpetuates stereotypes by selectively attending to confirming examples.
  • It discourages learning by filtering out information that challenges existing views.
  • It exacerbates conflict by reinforcing polarized viewpoints.
  • It amplifies misinformation by validating biased or incomplete sources.
  • It impairs scientific inquiry by influencing the interpretation of data.
  • It undermines collaboration by dismissing diverse perspectives.
  • It promotes poor financial or business decisions by focusing on validating data only.
  • It reduces adaptability to changing circumstances.

 

Curse of Knowledge

Curse of Knowledge = the cognitive bias where individuals who are well-informed or experienced in a specific topic struggle to consider the perspective of others who lack the same knowledge. It leads to difficulty in effectively communicating, teaching, or empathizing with less knowledgeable individuals because the expert assumes that others share their understanding.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We train employees to adopt a beginner’s mindset when communicating complex ideas.
  • We encourage the use of plain language and relatable analogies in all explanations.
  • We provide resources and templates for creating clear, accessible presentations and documentation.
  • We create mentorship programs that include training on effective teaching and communication.
  • We encourage asking clarifying questions to uncover assumptions and ensure shared understanding.
  • We use storytelling techniques to make complex information more engaging and relatable.
  • We offer tools and frameworks to help employees assess the knowledge levels of their audience.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It leads to overly complex explanations that confuse others.
  • It alienates less experienced individuals in discussions.
  • It reduces the ability to gauge others' learning needs.
  • It results in poorly designed instructions or materials that lack clarity.
  • It creates a gap in empathy for beginners or new learners.
  • It increases frustration in team environments where different expertise levels exist.
  • It limits the success of knowledge transfer in collaborative projects.
  • It limits effective collaboration by failing to clarify key details.
  • It weakens feedback by assuming others know what corrections are needed.
  • It can alienate clients or customers in professional interactions.
  • It creates barriers to effective leadership by overestimating others’ capabilities.

 

Dunning-Kruger Effect

Dunning-Kruger Effect = the cognitive bias where individuals with limited knowledge or skills in a specific domain overestimate their competence, while highly skilled individuals may undervalue their expertise. It arises from an inability to recognize the scope of one's own ignorance or expertise, often leading to inflated confidence or unnecessary self-doubt in various contexts.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We provide regular training and assessments to help employees accurately gauge their skill levels.
  • We promote collaborative decision-making, ensuring input from skilled experts is prioritized.
  • We establish clear performance metrics to differentiate confidence from demonstrated competence.
  • We conduct periodic self-assessment workshops paired with peer reviews for balanced feedback.
  • We celebrate contributions based on merit, ensuring competence is recognized over bravado.
  • We integrate tools that provide objective insights into individual and team performance.
  • We set realistic expectations by clearly defining roles, responsibilities, and qualifications.
  • We promote the expertise of skilled individuals to build their confidence and elevate their contributions.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It encourages overconfidence in those who lack the necessary skills.
  • It leads to poor decision-making based on inaccurate self-assessment.
  • It hinders learning by creating the illusion of competence.
  • It discourages feedback-seeking behavior among the least skilled.
  • It undermines collaboration when unqualified individuals dominate discussions.
  • It creates frustration for experts when their knowledge is undervalued.
  • It reduces team effectiveness by misaligning tasks with actual capabilities.
  • It causes unqualified individuals to take on roles they are unprepared for.
  • It fosters unrealistic expectations about performance outcomes.
  • It perpetuates mediocrity by rewarding confidence over competence.
  • It impairs learning by reducing the motivation to seek improvement.

 

False Consensus Effect

False Consensus Effect = the cognitive bias where individuals overestimate the extent to which others share their beliefs, opinions, values, or behaviors. It results from overgeneralizing one's own perspective as representative of the group or society, often leading to flawed assumptions, ineffective communication, and misaligned expectations in various social and professional scenarios.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We implement structured discussions where employees explicitly state their perspectives rather than assuming consensus.
  • We use anonymous surveys and feedback tools to capture the true range of opinions and avoid overestimating agreement.
  • We require teams to document and validate the reasoning behind decisions, checking for untested assumptions.
  • We assign roles such as "contrarian" in meetings to deliberately introduce alternative viewpoints.
  • We provide tools to analyze data objectively, reducing reliance on personal perspectives in decision-making.
  • We encourage the use of external benchmarking and third-party validation to counter internal echo chambers.
  • We establish norms for checking assumptions explicitly during project kickoffs and strategy sessions.
  • We evaluate team dynamics periodically to ensure diverse voices are not being overshadowed by assumed agreement.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It creates misunderstandings by assuming agreement where none exists.
  • It distorts social dynamics by underestimating diversity of thought.
  • It leads to overconfidence in decision-making based on assumed alignment.
  • It causes frustration when expected support from others is not received.
  • It undermines leadership by failing to address differing team viewpoints.
  • It reduces trust when others feel their perspectives are misrepresented.
  • It impairs strategic planning by overestimating shared goals.
  • It increases risk of project failure by ignoring potential objections.
  • It alienates stakeholders when their differing priorities are overlooked.
  • It reduces team morale when minority views are ignored.
  • It discourages feedback-seeking behavior by assuming agreement.

 

Framing Effect

Framing Effect = the cognitive bias where people's decisions and judgments are influenced by how information is presented, rather than the content itself. It emphasizes the power of context, language, or structure in shaping perceptions, often leading individuals to react differently to the same information depending on whether it is framed positively or negatively.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We implement decision-making frameworks that require evaluation of the same data in multiple contexts.
  • We standardize reporting templates to reduce the influence of selective framing in data presentation.
  • We promote critical thinking through workshops that highlight how framing impacts perceptions.
  • We require justification for key decisions based on data rather than presentation style.
  • We train employees to recognize and challenge framing techniques in communication.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It distorts judgment by presenting outcomes in a biased manner.
  • It reduces objectivity by focusing on the wording rather than the substance.
  • It encourages irrational choices based on emotional framing.
  • It impairs problem-solving by narrowing focus to the framed context.
  • It distorts memory by aligning recall with the original framing.
  • It amplifies bias when frames reinforce preexisting beliefs.
  • It reduces learning opportunities by masking the full context.

 

Halo Effect

Halo Effect = the cognitive bias where an overall positive or negative impression of a person, product, or entity disproportionately influences judgments about their other characteristics, even in unrelated areas. This bias often leads to overly favorable or unfavorable assessments, clouding objectivity and critical thinking.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We use structured evaluation criteria to ensure decisions are based on measurable factors rather than overall impressions.
  • We separate evaluations into distinct categories to assess performance, behavior, and other attributes independently.
  • We implement blind assessments where possible, focusing on outcomes rather than personalities.
  • We encourage teams to provide specific examples to justify their evaluations and decisions.
  • We rotate roles in peer evaluations to reduce favoritism and provide fresh viewpoints.
  • We use performance data and evidence-based metrics to guide decisions about promotions and rewards.
  • We implement policies requiring justification for high-stakes decisions to ensure they’re grounded in facts.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It causes inaccurate assessments by generalizing from a single trait.
  • It fosters unfair treatment of individuals based on initial impressions.
  • It distorts hiring decisions by overemphasizing first impressions.
  • It hinders critical analysis of a leader’s actual competence.
  • It encourages unrealistic expectations for those with high social appeal.
  • It discourages recognition of flaws in well-liked individuals.
  • It skews evaluations of performance, overrating certain individuals.
  • It diminishes fairness in peer reviews or team settings.
  • It oversimplifies judgments by anchoring on a single quality.
  • It weakens constructive criticism by overvaluing personal appeal.
  • It perpetuates stereotypes by associating unrelated traits with perceived attractiveness.

 

Illusory Truth Effect

Illusory Truth Effect = the cognitive bias where repeated exposure to a statement increases its perceived truthfulness, regardless of its accuracy. This bias arises because familiarity is mistaken for credibility, often leading individuals to accept false or misleading information as true simply because it feels familiar or comfortable.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We maintain a central knowledge repository to ensure that accurate, vetted information is accessible to all.
  • We use tools to track and audit key communications, ensuring consistency and accuracy over time.
  • We review and update training materials regularly to prevent the perpetuation of inaccuracies.
  • We promote cross-departmental collaboration to prevent echo chambers where misinformation can thrive.
  • We integrate fact-checking into our project kickoff processes to ensure sound starting assumptions.
  • We regularly review key workflows and practices to confirm their effectiveness and relevance.
  • We ensure performance reviews are evidence-based, avoiding reliance on repeated opinions or hearsay.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It reinforces misinformation through repeated exposure.
  • It perpetuates false beliefs in media and advertising.
  • It creates barriers to debunking myths or correcting errors.
  • It amplifies the spread of propaganda or biased narratives.
  • It diminishes the impact of new, accurate information.
  • It reinforces stereotypes by making repeated associations feel valid.
  • It encourages reliance on oversimplified or repeated explanations.
  • It increases difficulty in unlearning ingrained misconceptions.
  • It undermines innovation by reinforcing outdated practices as truths.
  • It amplifies the influence of dominant voices repeating the same messages.
  • It perpetuates inefficiencies by normalizing familiar but ineffective workflows.
  • It creates bias in performance reviews by relying on repeated impressions.

 

Observer-Expectancy Effect

Observer-Expectancy Effect = the cognitive bias where an observer’s expectations or beliefs about an outcome inadvertently influence the behavior of those being observed, often through subtle, unconscious cues. This effect can distort the validity of observations, lead to self-fulfilling prophecies, and compromise the accuracy of research or evaluations.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We standardize interview and evaluation processes to ensure fairness and consistency across candidates.
  • We use objective metrics and data-driven tools to guide decision-making and performance assessments.
  • We ensure diverse participation in evaluations and research to balance individual expectations.
  • We incorporate third-party reviewers for critical evaluations to validate outcomes independently.
  • We establish clear guidelines for providing feedback to avoid unintentional shaping of behavior.
  • We educate managers on the risks of projecting expectations onto teams, especially during performance reviews.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It alters participant behavior in experiments through non-verbal cues from researchers.
  • It affects survey results by leading respondents toward anticipated answers.
  • It undermines objectivity in performance reviews by shaping employee behaviors during evaluations.
  • It reinforces workplace hierarchies by amplifying behaviors aligned with leadership assumptions.
  • It impairs mentoring by subtly nudging mentees to meet predefined expectations instead of fostering growth.
  • It amplifies miscommunication during interviews by unintentionally signaling preferred responses.

 

Peak–End Rule

Peak–End Rule = the cognitive bias where people judge an experience largely based on how they felt at its most intense moment (the peak) and its conclusion (the end), rather than on the overall experience. It affects memory, decision-making, and satisfaction, leading to evaluations that do not reflect the entirety of the experience.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We use performance reviews that consider consistency and long-term contributions alongside peaks.
  • We gather feedback throughout the process in training programs, not just at the conclusion.
  • We implement balanced recognition programs that reward sustained performance, not just dramatic achievements.
  • We ensure project retrospectives include analysis of the full lifecycle, not just the delivery phase.
  • We collect feedback at regular intervals to capture a complete view of experiences, avoiding bias toward endings.
  • We create policies for awarding bonuses that account for sustained effort and consistent results.
  • We implement continuous performance tracking systems to provide a more accurate view of contributions.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It distorts performance reviews by focusing on a single high or low point.
  • It creates biased employee feedback by emphasizing the end of a project over its process.
  • It undermines workplace training evaluations by ignoring the overall learning experience.
  • It influences hiring decisions by overvaluing standout moments in interviews.
  • It fosters unfair recognition by prioritizing climactic achievements over consistent performance.
  • It misguides project retrospectives by focusing on the final delivery rather than the entire lifecycle.
  • It impacts client relationships by overvaluing the last interaction in ongoing engagements.
  • It undermines long-term planning by focusing on recent successes or setbacks.
  • It affects workplace policies by overreacting to memorable successes or failures.

 

Planning Fallacy

Planning Fallacy = the cognitive bias where individuals underestimate the time, costs, or risks associated with completing a task, while overestimating their ability to complete it efficiently. It leads to overly optimistic timelines and resource planning, often resulting in delays, budget overruns, and unmet expectations.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We encourage the use of historical data and past project benchmarks to create realistic timelines and budgets.
  • We implement structured planning frameworks that require detailed breakdowns of tasks and milestones.
  • We build buffer time into project schedules to accommodate unforeseen challenges.
  • We encourage iterative planning, allowing adjustments based on new information or changing circumstances.
  • We involve cross-functional teams in the planning process to account for all dependencies and complexities.
  • We require regular check-ins and progress reviews to ensure projects remain on track and adjustments are made proactively.
  • We use project management tools that provide visibility into task durations and resource availability.
  • We implement scenario planning to explore best-case, worst-case, and most-likely outcomes.
  • We provide training in time management and project estimation techniques for all employees.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It causes project delays by underestimating the time required for completion.
  • It leads to budget overruns by overlooking hidden or unexpected costs.
  • It fosters unrealistic timelines that strain team morale.
  • It creates inefficiencies by underestimating task complexities.
  • It leads to rushed decisions as deadlines approach unexpectedly.
  • It impacts team dynamics by placing excessive pressure on employees.
  • It discourages contingency planning by overconfidence in initial estimates.
  • It fosters overcommitment, leading to burnout and reduced productivity.
  • It distorts workload management by underestimating task durations.
  • It undermines leadership credibility when plans fail repeatedly.
  • It discourages accurate reflection on past projects when optimism clouds post-mortem analyses.

 

Self-Serving Bias

Self-Serving Bias = the cognitive bias where individuals attribute their successes to personal qualities or efforts while blaming external factors for their failures. It inflates self-esteem but distorts reality, leading to challenges in accountability, learning, and collaboration in various contexts.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We implement objective performance evaluation systems that assess contributions based on measurable outcomes.
  • We encourage feedback loops where peers and leaders provide constructive insights into individual and team performance.
  • We introduce blame-free environments where mistakes are treated as shared learning opportunities to reduce defensiveness.
  • We use balanced scorecards for performance reviews, which separate individual effort from team and external factors.
  • We require fact-based retrospectives for all projects, focusing on evidence rather than subjective narratives.
  • We provide confidential coaching sessions to help employees gain awareness of their biases and develop strategies for objective reflection.
  • We ensure 360-degree feedback mechanisms include questions that assess how individuals share credit and take responsibility.
  • We require pre-mortems and post-mortems on significant projects to explore potential biases before and after execution.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It leads to overconfidence, resulting in riskier decisions or strategies.
  • It reduces team trust by consistently deflecting blame onto others.
  • It creates a culture of defensiveness where accountability is avoided.
  • It distorts workplace accountability by shifting blame onto external factors.
  • It reduces learning opportunities by dismissing failures as circumstantial.
  • It undermines constructive feedback by deflecting responsibility.
  • It distorts leadership evaluations by overemphasizing successes while ignoring leadership shortcomings.
  • It amplifies workplace tensions by fostering perceptions of unfairness.
  • It perpetuates misunderstandings by misrepresenting the causes of success or failure.
  • It diminishes organizational learning by ignoring systemic issues in favor of external blame.

 

Social Desirability Bias

Social Desirability Bias = the cognitive bias where individuals tend to present themselves in a way they believe will be viewed favorably by others, often leading to inaccurate self-reporting or behavior that aligns with perceived social norms. It distorts data collection, decision-making, and interpersonal dynamics by prioritizing acceptance over authenticity.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We provide alternative channels for feedback, such as one-on-one check-ins or confidential forms, to reduce pressure to conform.
  • We conduct blind brainstorming sessions to prioritize ideas over individual reputations or perceived norms.
  • We use psychometric tools in engagement surveys to detect patterns indicative of overly positive responses.
  • We use indirect questioning techniques in surveys, asking about general trends or group perceptions rather than individual opinions.
  • We partner with external consultants to conduct cultural audits, providing employees with a neutral platform for honest feedback.
  • We introduce gamified feedback tools to make the process engaging while reducing the pressure to provide socially desirable responses.
  • We conduct frequent, small-scale pulse surveys to capture evolving sentiments without placing excessive weight on any single response.
  • We include behavioral questions in feedback forms, focusing on observed actions rather than personal judgments.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It skews survey results by encouraging respondents to provide socially approved answers.
  • It undermines workplace feedback by discouraging honest critiques.
  • It reduces the accuracy of performance evaluations when employees self-assess.
  • It creates false perceptions of alignment with company values or culture.
  • It discourages innovation by prioritizing popular ideas over bold or unconventional ones.
  • It undermines personal growth by promoting self-presentation over self-reflection.
  • It fosters an unrealistic perception of employee satisfaction or well-being.
  • It creates challenges in diversity and inclusion initiatives by concealing biases or prejudices.
  • It perpetuates workplace inefficiencies by avoiding open discussions about systemic issues.

 

Sunk Cost Fallacy

Sunk Cost Fallacy = the cognitive bias where individuals continue to invest time, effort, or resources in a decision or project because of prior investments, rather than considering the current value or future benefits. It often results in irrational persistence and prevents more effective alternatives from being pursued.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We encourage regular project reviews to assess current viability and alignment with organizational goals.
  • We establish clear exit criteria for projects to determine when to pivot, pause, or discontinue efforts.
  • We promote a culture where abandoning nonviable projects is seen as a strategic move, not a failure.
  • We emphasize data-driven decision-making to prioritize future benefits over past investments.
  • We use scenario planning to compare the potential outcomes of continuing versus abandoning efforts.
  • We involve diverse stakeholders in project assessments to ensure balanced perspectives on persistence or change.
  • We set regular checkpoints for resource allocation, allowing re-evaluation of ongoing projects.
  • We celebrate examples of successful pivots and highlight lessons learned from discontinuing nonviable projects.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It encourages continued investment in failing projects to justify prior expenditures.
  • It discourages pivoting to better opportunities by emphasizing past commitments.
  • It wastes resources on projects that no longer align with organizational goals.
  • It fosters resistance to change by emphasizing previous efforts.
  • It reduces agility by locking resources into legacy systems or initiatives.
  • It inflates operational costs by refusing to phase out underperforming initiatives.
  • It fosters resentment among employees forced to work on nonviable tasks.
  • It increases burnout when employees are pressured to rescue doomed projects.
  • It keeps the business tied to underperforming vendors or partners.
  • It distorts performance evaluations by overvaluing persistence over outcomes.
  • It impacts strategic planning by anchoring decisions to previous investments.

 

Zero-Sum Bias

Zero-Sum Bias = the cognitive bias where individuals mistakenly believe that one person's gain inevitably results in another's loss. It assumes a fixed amount of resources or benefits, disregarding opportunities for mutual growth or shared success.

How we prevent this bias:

  • We create resource-allocation models that emphasize expansion, innovation, and shared benefits rather than finite distribution.
  • We reward creative solutions that maximize value for all stakeholders, reinforcing the benefits of non-competitive approaches.
  • We integrate feedback loops to evaluate how often and effectively employees find collaborative rather than divisive solutions.
  • We implement decision-making frameworks, such as integrative negotiation techniques, that prioritize mutual gain over competition.
  • We ensure company-wide communications consistently emphasize abundance, shared growth, and expanding opportunities.
  • We appoint neutral facilitators for resource discussions to keep the focus on organization-wide benefits.
  • We invest in training programs focused on problem-solving and negotiation techniques that uncover win-win scenarios.
Don't

Drawbacks:

  • It hinders teamwork by fostering unnecessary competition among colleagues.
  • It skews negotiations by prioritizing short-term wins over long-term partnerships.
  • It limits innovation by discouraging shared efforts to create new value.
  • It discourages knowledge sharing, fearing others will gain an edge.
  • It creates conflict in resource allocation by assuming fixed limitations.
  • It misguides project prioritization by focusing on rivalries rather than outcomes.
  • It stifles organizational growth by failing to leverage synergies between teams.
  • It discourages optimism, fostering a culture of scarcity rather than abundance.
